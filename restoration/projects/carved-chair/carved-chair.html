Initial Assessments & Dating

  • Spray painted gold unevenly and sloppily.
  • The back rest design looks very hand carved.
  • The legs and struts look hand turned and imperfect.
  • Definitely not mass produced - very hand-made.
  • The bottom used to be cane-woven, but all the cane except for wedged-in dregs were left.
  • Inset drilled seat frame.
  • A piece of thin plywood was cut to cover the seat. This wood was traced with pencil in a way that suggests the owner intended to cut it to exact size to fit the hole. This wood was also spray painted gold.
  • The holes for (hand-caned, not pre-woven insert) cane weaving are inset- unusual.
  • The wood that would be under the cane weaving was painted black. I believe this is orginial. It is worn but still there. It was to bring out the contrast and not detract from the woven look. Luckily, this area was spared from the gold paint.
  • Uneven tool marks and asymmetry in wood turning.
  • No nails or screws- only hand-cut wooden dowels.

A safe and supported all-around estimate: 1845-1860 (maybe shifting toward the earlier side of that)

Chat GPT's reasoning:
1. Hand-Carved and Hand-Turned Construction
The imperfect symmetry and visible tool marks on both the backrest carving and turned legs indicate pre-industrial craftsmanship.
By the 1840s–1850s, machine turning was common in large workshops, but many rural and small-town cabinetmakers still hand-turned components, especially in the South and along the East Coast.
The lack of perfect uniformity suggests bench-made rather than factory-made production.
2. Caned Seat (Hand-Woven, Not Drop-In)
Hand-caned seats were very common before the 1870s, when slip-in or machine-pressed cane panels began to dominate.
The fact that it’s a hole-to-hole woven seat (as opposed to a pre-woven panel) strongly suggests mid-19th century or earlier.
Before ~1840: most seats were completely flat on top; the holes went straight through a level frame. The binding cane had to be tied or pinned down manually.
After ~1845–1850: makers started routing or chiseling a shallow inset to make the finished seat appear more “flush” and protect the caning.
By ~1860–1870: this became a hallmark of factory-made “fancy chairs” and improved handmade versions influenced by factory designs.
3. Joinery and Fasteners
You mentioned no nails or screws, and wooden pegs or dowels instead. That’s a major pre-1870 indicator.
By the 1880s, most furniture—even decorative chairs—relied on some metal fasteners.
4. Paint and Finish Layers
The old black paint under the seat could be lampblack pigment in linseed oil—a common 1840s–1860s protective treatment for hidden wood surfaces.
The black underlayer being hard, thin, and matte fits 19th-century finishes (often used to seal unseen areas).
5. Style and Form
Without seeing the exact silhouette, the combination of cane seat + carved back + turned legs fits within the transitional period between late Federal and early Victorian designs.
These were often called “cottage chairs,” “fancy chairs,” or “parlor chairs.”
The craftsmanship leans toward vernacular or regional work, not factory-made. Given you’re in South Carolina, it could easily be Southern-made, possibly by a local cabinetmaker.

Restoration Process

Overview

  • CitruStrip was applied to almost the whole thing in pieces. I chose this because I heard CitruStrip was more respectful to antique wood than harsher paint strippers.
  • I was using Clean Strip to clean off the paint stripper (after scraping off most of it) and discovered that the Clean Strip was actually effective at stripping off the paint by itsself. After discovering that, much of the wood was stripped that way.
  • Using Clean Strip, I realized there was a bit of old wood finish, in the color walnut I would say (dark-medium brown).
  • Through stripping, I also learned that it was at some point painted off-white or white (why?), then stripped, then painted gold. Almost like someone used to care about it and then things went downhill. The dregs of white paint were recalcitrant and deep in crevices- hard to remove. Clean Strip didn't have an effect. It had to be picked and scraped off.
  • Using a small cleaning brush during the CitruStrip phase was VERY effective on the hand-carved back panel. No picking required!
  • After it was all stripped, I coated the original black paint with Renaissance Wax for protection.
  • I used Howard's Restor-A-Finish in Walnut.
  • I used Howard's Feed & Wax.
  • I bought a chair cane weaving kit on Amazon. The cane is 2.5mm.

1830-1855 Dresser

Purchase Info

This dresser was bought by me, Kalyn Morris, in September 2025 for $75.00. It was bought at Antiques at Hall Lumber Co in Chester, SC (106 Hudson St, Chester, SC 29706). The owner Rhett told me that his wife's relative owned this desk until her death. Her name was Mary Poteat and she lived in Marion, NC. She got a doctorate degree from Duke. She lived to be 96. He told me she would be 110-115 years old now, as of 2025. That would mean she must have been born 1910-1915. It would also imply she died around 2006-2011. Rhett does not know where she got the dresser.

Initial Assessments & Dating

  • The dresser had to be cleaned extensively all over, and a lot of dirt came off, built up over time. Some kind of small stuck-on white paint splatters.
  • General markings
  • Stripped finish in places.
  • Dull finish.
  • Piercing squeak when drawers are opened from raw wood-on-wood tracks.
  • Tracks have beautiful visible file marks.
  • Missing track under the bottom drawer.
  • The backing of the dresser was replaced in the 1930’s-1960’s (likely the 1950’s), a common practice for these old dressers. The sticker on the board says “Weldwood.” Weldwood plywood was introduced by United States Plywood Corporation in the late 1930s and widely marketed through the 1950s–60s as waterproof paneling/backing.
  • Hand-cut dovetail joinery- typically pre-1860. Later 19th century dovetails become more machine-regular. The locks are Corbin locks. Corbin Cabinet Lock Co. Company active from 1849 onward. The shinier plating might indicate a later replacement.
  • Diamond-inset keyhole escutcheon- this was a style element seen in Federal/Empire through early Victorian periods (c. 1820–1860). Nice!!
  • Wood looks like Poplar, I’m unsure. Poplar is consistent with domestic, utilitarian furniture of the time, whether finished plainly or intended for paint or veneer.
  • Slightly asymmetrical filigree drawer pulls. Repeating asymmetry with visible file marks- early sand-cast. The fact that they are slightly irregular and hand-finished suggests pre-1860 manufacture.
  • Flathead screws with round scratches on them. Looks like they’re cut nails, machine-cut but finished by hand filing, common ca. 1790s–1880s. Since they are flathead, irregular, and show hand filing marks, they’re probably in the 1830–1860 window.
  • Each drawer is a different height.
  • Wood work is hand-crafted- beautiful imperfections in every detail, such as the carved keyholes and joinery.
  • Different drawer heights, imperfections, file marks on tracks - these are hallmarks of true hand craftsmanship, not machine shop furniture.

A safe and supported all-around estimate: 1830–1855 American dresser (Empire transitioning into early Victorian).

Restoration Process

The restoration was carried out in accordance with professional conservation practices, with careful attention to preserving the dresser’s historical integrity. No artificial finishes, heavy sanding, or modern substitutions were employed.

  • The entire case and drawers were first cleaned with a mild vinegar-and-water solution to remove accumulated surface dirt without compromising the original finish.
  • I picked off the paint splatters entirely by hand.
  • Howard’s Restor-A-Finish (Walnut) was applied. This blended out minor surface abrasions, white marks, and areas of worn finish, restoring continuity of color.
  • To condition and nourish the wood, Howard’s Feed & Wax was applied, enriching the luster and adding a protective layer.
  • All brass hardware, which retains its original sand-cast character, was treated with Von Schneider’s micro-crystalline wax polish (Renaissance Wax), ensuring protection against tarnish without the loss of historic surface detail.
  • The characteristic squeak of the raw wood-on-wood drawer runners was resolved by applying Renaissance Wax to the points of contact. This provided lubrication while respecting the original unfinished interiors.
  • Wood now has restored depth of color, minimized or erased abrasions or white marks, and is nourished and protected.
  • I made custom covers out of a thrifted tablecloth. I washed it and cut it to size. It is NOT glued and is completely removable. It’s all tied into place at the back of the drawers to prevent sliding.
